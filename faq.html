The Basics
What triggers should I avoid?

Based on my own 23-year self-observation, two dietary categories consistently drive my chronic acne and rosacea:

Neu5Gc — a sugar molecule found in red meat and dairy. ATIs and gliadin — proteins found in wheat, separate from gluten sensitivity or celiac disease.

Eliminating both is what cleared my skin, every time, for over two decades. Reintroducing either one reliably brought the flare-ups back.

This is my own documented pattern, not a clinical guarantee that it works the same way for everyone. See the white papers for the full mechanism I propose.

Is this actually proven, or is it just anecdotal?

It's not a peer-reviewed clinical trial — I want to be upfront about that. What it is: a 23-year, self-documented pattern of elimination and reintroduction, repeated many times over, with consistent results each time.

I've also corresponded with researchers at UCSD, including Dr. Ajit Varki and Dr. Nissi Varki, whose published research on Neu5Gc biology forms part of the scientific foundation for the hypothesis.

The white papers lay out both the mechanism I propose and its current evidentiary limits honestly. I'd rather you know exactly what this is and isn't before you decide whether to test it yourself.

Where do I even start?

The cheapest way to find out if this applies to you: eliminate red meat, dairy, and wheat for 4–6 weeks. Track your skin. Then reintroduce one category at a time, a few weeks apart, and watch what happens.

That's essentially what I stumbled into 23 years ago on a modified Atkins diet — I just didn't know why it was working until later.

The Bigger Picture
Does this only apply to acne and rosacea?

My own evidence base is acne and rosacea — that's what I've lived and documented for 23 years. But the underlying hypothesis (which I call the Sialic-Glycan Inflammatory Cascade, or SGIC) proposes the same dietary triggers may play a role in a wider range of chronic inflammatory conditions.

I've written separate white papers exploring possible connections to conditions like fibromyalgia, FSHD, arthritis, and others. These are more exploratory than the acne/rosacea work, and I try to be clear about that distinction in the papers themselves.
