What Is Neu5Gc?

A plain-language guide to the meat sugar your body treats as foreign — and why a retired nurse spent 23 years chasing it.

By Martin Hamer, RN (Ret.)• ~10 min read• Last reviewed July 2026

There is a sugar molecule that almost every mammal on Earth makes in its own body — cows, pigs, sheep, deer, dogs, cats. Humans are one of the rare exceptions. We lost the ability to make it somewhere around three million years ago, and we never got it back.

Its name is N-glycolylneuraminic acid, mercifully shortened to Neu5Gc (say “new-five-gee-see”). And here is the strange part: even though our bodies stopped making it, we never stopped eating it. It is still in the red meat and dairy on our plates. So it gets into us anyway — and our immune system, which has spent millions of years learning that this molecule is not one of ours, notices.

This article explains what Neu5Gc is, why the human relationship with it is so unusual, and what the established science says happens when we eat it. At the end, I’ll be clear about where that established science stops and where my own hypothesis — the reason this whole website exists — begins.

In one sentence

Neu5Gc is a sugar made by most mammals but not by humans; we absorb it from red meat and dairy, our immune system makes antibodies against it, and the resulting low-grade inflammation is a well-documented feature of human biology.

The three-million-year-old typo

Sialic acids are a family of sugars that sit on the outermost surface of nearly every cell in your body — the very tips of the molecular “fuzz” that coats your cells. They are among the first things any other cell, or any microbe, or your own immune system, actually touches. Most mammals carry two closely related versions: Neu5Ac and Neu5Gc. The body builds Neu5Gc from Neu5Ac using a single enzyme.

In the human lineage, the gene for that enzyme — called CMAH — broke. A piece of it was deleted, leaving the instructions unreadable. Every human being alive carries this same inactivating change; it is one of the clearest genetic lines separating us from our closest primate relatives. The result is simple to state: humans cannot make Neu5Gc. Our cell surfaces are built almost entirely from the other version, Neu5Ac.

For most of human history that would have been the end of the story. A gene switched off; a molecule we no longer carry. But we are omnivores, and much of what we eat is other mammals.

Still on the menu

Here is the twist that makes Neu5Gc worth an entire field of research. Although we can’t manufacture it, our cells can’t tell the difference between the two sugars when they’re recycling raw materials from food. So when you eat Neu5Gc, your body quietly takes it up through a salvage pathway and installs it onto your own cell surfaces — as if it belonged there.

The richest dietary sources are, by a wide margin, red meats — beef, pork, lamb — and dairy. Poultry and most fish carry little to none, which is one reason those foods behave so differently in this framework. Because it enters through ordinary metabolism, this incorporation begins early: the antibodies discussed below tend to appear in the first months of life, coinciding with the introduction of red meat and cow’s-milk formula.

So the human body ends up in a genuinely odd position. It is displaying, on its own cells, a molecule that its immune system has evolved to regard as not human.

A molecule we cannot make, worn on cells we cannot disown.

The part your immune system objects to

Because Neu5Gc is foreign to us, the immune system does what it does with foreign things: it makes antibodies. These are unusual antibodies, though. They are directed against a molecule that is now sitting on our own tissues — so researchers gave them a fittingly awkward name: “xeno-autoantibodies.” Xeno for foreign, auto for self. Foreign-yet-self.

Every human tested carries these anti-Neu5Gc antibodies, though the amounts and the exact targets vary a great deal from person to person. And this sets up a slow, quiet friction: dietary Neu5Gc keeps getting deposited into tissues, and circulating antibodies keep recognising it. The interaction between the two produces chronic, low-grade inflammation. The Varki laboratory at UC San Diego — whose foundational work underlies everything on this site, and with whom I’ve corresponded for over two decades — coined a word for it: xenosialitis.

Established science

That humans lost CMAH and cannot synthesise Neu5Gc; that dietary Neu5Gc from red meat and dairy is incorporated into human tissues; that all humans carry anti-Neu5Gc antibodies; and that the resulting “xenosialitis” produces chronic inflammation — all of this is peer-reviewed, replicated science. In human-like mice engineered to share our Neu5Gc deficiency, this same antibody interaction has been shown to promote conditions such as atherosclerosis and to accelerate certain cancers. References are listed at the foot of this article.

Why this matters beyond the laboratory

Chronic low-grade inflammation is not a dramatic, obvious thing. It doesn’t announce itself the way a broken bone or a fever does. It is the slow background hum increasingly implicated across a long list of modern conditions — cardiovascular disease, certain cancers, metabolic disease. Neu5Gc is one identified, dietary, modifiable contributor to that hum. You choose, three times a day, how much of it you take in.

That last point is what caught my attention as a nurse, and never let go. Most of the inflammatory drivers we talk about in medicine are things you can’t easily change — your genes, your age, your history. This one you can put down or pick up at the grocery store.

Where the established science ends — and where I come in

Everything above is mainstream, published biology. I want to be scrupulous about the line I’m about to cross, because keeping it clear is a matter of principle for me.

In 2003, following a modified Atkins diet, I watched a chronic skin condition I’d had for years simply resolve. Careful re-testing on myself confirmed a reproducible pattern: red meat and dairy reliably brought it back within hours; removing them cleared it again. Years later I watched Dr. Ajit Varki describe the universal human immune response to Neu5Gc on a CBS broadcast, and a private observation suddenly had a plausible mechanism behind it.

My hypothesis — not established fact

The Sialic-Glycan Inflammatory Cascade (SGIC), or Hamer Loop, is my proposal that this same xenosialitis mechanism — often working together with a second signal from wheat proteins called ATIs and gliadins — is an under-recognised driver of chronic inflammatory conditions dermatology and general medicine have struggled with, including acne and rosacea.

This is a hypothesis under active investigation. It is built on established Neu5Gc science, decades of clinical observation, and a reproducible personal record — but it has not been proven in controlled trials, and I am an independent researcher, not a dermatologist or immunologist. I hold it as a serious, testable idea, not a settled conclusion. That distinction runs through everything I publish.

The rest of this library is my attempt to lay that case out honestly: what the science firmly supports, what I’m proposing on top of it, and how you could test any of it for yourself. If you want the mechanism in full, the white paper corpus is the place to go. If you’d rather start with something practical, the free Acnerase app scans food labels for hidden Neu5Gc sources.
